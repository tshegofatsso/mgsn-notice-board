2018 · THE BEGINNING

Sixty people from Mabopane
gathered under the trees at Block M.

Not because someone told them to. Not because there was funding. Because the gum trees were drinking the river dry and nobody had done anything about it yet.

Five rows of identification sheets were laid on the stone table. Solanum mauritianum. Bugweed. Pom pom grass. Lantana camara. The community read the names the way you read a warning — because that is exactly what it was.

"Ke nna thata go tshwana ga go sa dire" — It is more frightening to stay silent while the river dies than to speak up.

THE WORK

Nna ke ya hae.
"I belong here. This is mine to tend."

That is what MGSN means. Not a programme. Not a project. A declaration. Ganno ba tshwanela go nna moo — these are the rightful people of this place, and the land knows the difference.

The UP team — led by Ida, supported by Ketla and Tania — stood in that same riverbed and made the science legible to people who had lived next to that water their whole lives. Siegwalt the construction expert did not stop talking until every person in those blue chairs understood exactly what was happening beneath their feet.

And then the work started.

THE PEOPLE

Not a training. Not a consultation.
A transfer.

Knowledge moved from University of Pretoria labs into Mabopane hands. Not向下. Not as charity. As collaboration. The City of Tshwane's Department of Environmental Compliance was there because the community invited them to be there — not the other way around.

Danish students flew halfway across the world and stood in a riverbed that has no tourism brochure. CONSUS sent representatives who listened more than they spoke. The MGSN volunteers — the people who actually live there — drove every decision.

"Fa go se na bosiko, go se na botho" — Where there is no respect, there is no care. The river heard the difference that day.

THE SPECIES

Nine species. Nine problems.
Nine reasons the river is struggling.

Invasive — Priority Removal
Solanum mauritianum — Bugweed
High seed production. Forms dense stands. Allelopathic compounds suppress indigenous regrowth.
Invasive — Priority Removal
Pom Pom grass ( spp.)
Rapid spread along water margins. Reduces baseflow. Alters sediment character.
Invasive — Priority Removal
Lantana camara
Toxic to livestock. Replaces indigenous understorey. Fire-prone when mature.
Invasive — Priority Removal
Eucalyptus camaldulensis — Gum tree
Consumes 200–800 litres per tree per day. The river's biggest thief.
Invasive — Priority Removal
Acacia baileyana — Bailey's Wattle
Blue-grey foliage. Yellow flowers. Classification required for targeted removal.
Invasive — Priority Removal
Ipomoea sp. — Morning Glory
Climbing vine. Smothers indigenous trees. Requires careful manual extraction.
Indigenous Alternatives Recommended
Vachellia karroo
Sweet thorn — nitrogen fixer, indigenous, water-wise
Plumbago auriculata
Cape leadwort — hardy, indigenous, attracts pollinators
